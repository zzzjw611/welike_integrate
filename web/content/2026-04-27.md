## Background: an underrated 'demo-as-ad' company

HeyGen was founded in 2020 by Joshua Xu and Wayne Liang, both previously video/AI engineers at Snap and ByteDance respectively. The original product, called Movio, was an 'AI Avatar video generator' — turning a text script into a video of a digital human delivering it on camera. The category was already crowded: Synthesia (UK), D-ID (Israel), and Colossyan all started earlier.

But between 2023 and 2025, HeyGen drew a curve Synthesia couldn't: ARR went from ~$1M in early 2023 to $35M by mid-2024 to over $100M in 2025. Synthesia's growth visibly slowed in the same window. The gap isn't in the model — both companies trade wins on technical specs — it's in distribution. HeyGen designs every product iteration as a 'demo people want to share' and then lets those demos run organically on X, TikTok, and LinkedIn with zero ad budget. The two most iconic moments: an August 2023 video of Lionel Messi speaking 33 languages fluently hit 140M impressions on a single X post; and in 2024 a Jensen Huang digital avatar appeared as a GTC keynote easter egg, retweeted by Nvidia's official account.

## Run demos as ads, not as demos

The default industry product-launch SOP is: make a demo → write the launch post → push PR → run paid ads to drive traffic. HeyGen tore that up. Their logic: **the demo itself is the ad, so the demo's design goal is not 'showcase the feature' but 'make people want to share it'**.

That sounds rhetorical, but at the execution level it produces an entirely different set of decisions. Take the Messi video — at the time, HeyGen's multilingual lip-sync wasn't industry-best on every language (D-ID was more accurate in some). But Joshua's team chose Messi rather than any random demo subject because soccer is the only truly cross-language motif on Earth. One Messi video has an order of magnitude more distribution than 100 ordinary-person demos. They ran the same formula again and again — Sam Altman in multiple languages, the Jensen Huang avatar, Trump–Biden translations — every drop a 'public figure + multilingual' combination.

The second-order effect: because the demos themselves are running as ads, HeyGen barely needs to buy paid media. Joshua noted on a 2024 podcast that paid acquisition is 'less than 10%' of new users — the rest is organic and word-of-mouth. That means at the same ARR, HeyGen's marginal margins are dramatically higher than peers — which is why they support $100M ARR with only ~200 people.

### Companion play: turn 'public beta' into a viral payload

There's a hidden move HeyGen runs every release. When they ship a new feature (Avatar 4.0, Interactive Avatar), they open a '7-day watermark-free free trial' window specifically to let KOLs generate freely on X and TikTok. The content those KOLs produce then becomes the source library for the next launch event. In effect they're paying the cost of 'letting users do the advertising' instead of paying for 'official ad spend' — but the resulting content is higher quality, more credible, and reaches better-targeted audiences.

## Market the product with the product itself, don't talk about the product

Nearly 100% of the videos on HeyGen's website, official X, and official YouTube are generated by HeyGen itself. This isn't a gimmick — it's a hard commitment: **if even the official team isn't willing to use the product to do their own marketing, why should anyone else trust it?**

This principle creates three direct consequences. First, the team hits product bugs and limits before customers do — hairline rendering issues, long-video stutters, lip-sync drift — and fixes them at least one release ahead of customer reports. Second, every official marketing video is a real upper bound of what the product can do, so market expectations and product delivery never decouple. That's a rare asset in AI, where 'jaw-dropping demo, mediocre reality' is the industry norm. Third, the line between marketing and product blurs — HeyGen's marketing lead and PMs share a single backlog, because the next marketing campaign requires the next product feature.

The flipped lesson for Chinese peers: many companies treat 'outsource our official video production to an MCN' as a sign of professionalism. In the AI tools category, it's a negative signal. Suno, ElevenLabs, Runway, HeyGen — the fastest-ARR companies all keep their official channels in-house and shipped using their own product.

## The leverage of a Chinese founding team building prosumer tools in Silicon Valley

HeyGen is a textbook 'China R&D + US market' structure. Core engineering sits in Shenzhen and Beijing; sales and marketing operate from LA and San Francisco. In enterprise SaaS this is usually treated as a disadvantage (compliance review, data residency, customer trust). In prosumer / creator tooling it's a massive advantage — faster development, lower headcount cost, and roughly 2× the iteration tempo of a pure-Silicon-Valley team.

What's subtler is the founders' bilingual leverage itself. Joshua publishes long-form essays on X and LinkedIn but also goes on Chinese podcasts. A meaningful share of HeyGen's earliest seed users came from the Chinese AI media ecosystem (early coverage from QbitAI, Synced, etc.). This 'dual-circle cold start' is something neither a pure-American nor a pure-Chinese founder can pull off. Comparable peers from the same period: Captions ($500M valuation), Pika Labs (Chinese-founded), Manus (Chinese-founded) — Chinese-led AI video / creative tool companies have collectively run to the front of the pack in the past 18 months.

Worth noting: in 2024, HeyGen ran a deliberate 'de-China' PR move (when pressed by some media on data compliance), explicitly relocating the corporate entity to Singapore and the US. This wasn't reactive — it was the posture adjustment required to open the North American enterprise market. Looking back today, this same pattern is converging across the global expansion paths of Meshy (today's QbitAI report on 14× ARR growth) and PixVerse (today's Mango TV partnership): **R&D stays in China, the corporate entity registers offshore, and the market plays both sides**.

## Three transferable lessons from HeyGen

1. **A demo isn't a product demo, it's ad creative**. Before every release, the first question to answer is 'can this demo earn 10M plays on X' — then decide how to build it. A demo that earns plays is always 'a public motif + a technical wow moment', not a list of features.
2. **Official channels must be 100% self-produced**. If you build AI tools, outsourcing your official content to an agency is a negative signal. Merge the marketing lead's and PMs' backlog so marketing demand pulls product iteration.
3. **In prosumer / creator tooling, the China-US dual structure is an asset, not a liability**. R&D in China, brand HQ overseas, plus a founder who can speak in both language ecosystems — in 2026 this is still the most efficient configuration for going global. The precondition is that the founder is willing to spend half their time creating content.
