## Background: Granola is a notes app — the marketing surface is the note itself

Granola launched in 2023 with a counterintuitive bet for the meeting-notetaker category: don't send a bot to the call. Instead, the Mac app listens locally, transcribes in the background, and turns the transcript into structured notes you edit afterward. Co-founder Chris Pedregal had previously sold Socratic to Google in 2018. The team raised seed money from Lightspeed, then a Series B reportedly led by Spark Capital at around $250M. There is no enterprise sales team worth talking about, no bottom-of-funnel SEO play, no influencer program. The growth motion is one thing: people share their Granola notes with colleagues and clients, and the recipient sees a clean, branded artifact that ends with a soft prompt to try the tool. It's the same shape as Notion's wiki-template virality — but compressed into a 30-minute meeting cadence.

## Run the artifact as the ad, not the product

Most B2B SaaS treats the product UI as the surface area to market: feature pages, comparison tables, "see it in action" demos. Granola's surface area is a meeting note that already left the building. Every time a founder shares a "here's what we covered today" doc with an investor, a candidate, or a customer, the recipient is reading a Granola-formatted artifact — clean structure, action items at the top, attendee context, and a small footer that signals what produced it. The second-order effect is that the highest-leverage users (founders, exec assistants, partners at funds) double as the highest-distribution channel: each of them sends 5–15 of these artifacts per week to people who themselves take a lot of meetings. We don't have public DAU numbers, but the signal is in pricing power — the Series B at ~$250M valuation came without conventional pipeline metrics, on the strength of inbound from this artifact loop alone.

## No bot in the meeting is a marketing decision, not a product decision

The category convention — Otter, Fireflies, tl;dv, Read.ai — is to dial a bot into the call. That bot is recognizable to every other attendee, which creates a friction every founder has felt: "Is this being recorded? Should I say this?" Granola's local-only capture removes that question. The product framing is "privacy" but the marketing framing is recommend-ability. People will not enthusiastically tell their CEO, their board, or a customer "you should try this — it joins your meetings as a third participant." They will tell them "you should try this — your colleagues won't even know it's there." The friction Granola removed is the social cost of recommending the tool. That's also why Granola wins inside firms (VC partners, founder Slacks, exec teams) where the per-person taste-maker effect compounds: one partner adopting it puts it in front of every portfolio CEO they meet that week.

## Pick a beachhead whose meeting calendar is a distribution network

Granola's earliest beachhead was VCs and founders — a community of users whose calendars are unusually dense (10–20 meetings/week) and whose counterparties are themselves heavy meeting-takers. That isn't an accident. If your viral loop runs on shared artifacts, you want each user to generate as many shared artifacts as possible *and* to send them to other prospective users. A category director at a CPG company taking three internal meetings a week is the wrong wedge — a partner at Sequoia taking eighteen external meetings a week is the right one. Once the tool was the default among founders and operators, it crossed into adjacent high-meeting-density roles (consultants, PMs, exec assistants, sales leaders).

## Three transferable lessons from Granola

1. **Make the artifact, not the app, the marketing surface**. The thing your users send to other people is your real ad inventory — design it that way, with format, branding, and a soft conversion prompt baked in.
2. **Optimize for recommend-ability, not just utility**. A product can be 20% less powerful and grow 10x faster if it removes the social friction of telling someone else to try it.
3. **Beachhead by calendar density, not by ICP description**. For artifact-driven virality, the right early users aren't the segment with the biggest TAM — they're the segment whose daily workflow naturally produces the most shareable outputs to the most relevant next users.
