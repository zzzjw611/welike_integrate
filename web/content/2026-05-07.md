## Background: A Shenzhen-rooted team that built a US AI brand without ever buying ads

HeyGen, founded in 2020 by ex-Snap engineer Joshua Xu, makes AI avatar video — type a script, pick an avatar, get a photorealistic video in 40+ languages. The company started life as Movio in Shenzhen, rebranded to HeyGen, and moved its HQ to Los Angeles. It went from roughly $1M ARR in 2022 to ~$35M by late 2023, and pushed past $100M ARR in 2025 — one of the fastest software ramps of the AI generation. Benchmark led a Series A at around $500M valuation; later rounds carried the company past $1B. The product itself is plain. What's not plain is the GTM: HeyGen has spent very little on traditional paid marketing. Most of its inbound funnel runs through one channel — Joshua Xu's Twitter feed.

## Run demos as ads, not as demos

Most B2B SaaS companies treat product demos as something gated behind a "book a call" form. HeyGen treats them as the ad. The Tara translation clip — a Vancouver developer speaking fluent Mandarin about half a second after the original audio — was posted to Twitter in mid-2023; it racked up tens of millions of views within a week and pulled HeyGen from a quiet enterprise tool into a household AI name. The Bezos avatar clone followed the same pattern. None of these were paid promotions. They were just product output, posted by the founder, with one line of caption. The second-order effect: HeyGen never had to commission marketing creative, because every feature ship was already a shareable asset. By contrast, Synthesia — its closest Western competitor, now valued past $2B — runs traditional B2B motion: webinars, gated whitepapers, paid LinkedIn, brand campaigns. HeyGen's CAC structure is reportedly a fraction of that, with a self-serve funnel that closes most SMB and prosumer deals without a sales touch. The viral asset and the product release are the same artifact, shipped on the same day.

## Build the team in Shenzhen, ship the brand in English

HeyGen's engineering and research talent is split between China and the US, but the public-facing brand is unmistakably American: LA HQ, English-first product, US-style pricing, US podcast circuit. Joshua Xu speaks at every Western AI event he can get on. This dual structure is the unspoken arbitrage. Shenzhen brings cheaper, deeper computer-vision and GAN talent — a category where Chinese research has been competitive for a decade. LA brings the brand frame and the customer trust that a video-of-your-face product needs. Crucially, Xu localized the founder narrative completely: he posts, podcasts, and writes in English; HeyGen's voice on social never reads as translated. The lesson most Chinese-founded SaaS companies miss is that "global" doesn't mean culturally neutral — it means picking a home market and committing. HeyGen is a US brand built on a globally distributed team, not the other way around.

## Turn every output into a billboard

HeyGen ships every video with watermarking that's visible on free plans and removable on paid ones. The free tier is generous — multiple minutes of generation per month — and the watermark functions as ambient distribution. When a creator posts a HeyGen-made video to TikTok or LinkedIn, viewers see the brand. Combined with Xu's founder-Twitter pipeline of feature demos, the result is a system where every cohort of users is also a marketing channel. The retention math benefits too: by the time a user has produced enough output to want the watermark gone, switching to a competitor means rebuilding their avatar library.

## Three transferable lessons from HeyGen

1. **The product output is the ad.** If your AI product generates something visible, the job is to make every output shareable, watermarked, and good enough that users want to post it unprompted.
2. **Founder-as-channel beats brand-as-channel for AI products in 2026.** A live feed of demos closes more deals than a polished landing page; Joshua Xu's Twitter is the proof and the precedent.
3. **Pick a home market, not a global one.** HeyGen's bilingual team works because the brand commits to one cultural frame — half-and-half positioning would have killed both ends.
